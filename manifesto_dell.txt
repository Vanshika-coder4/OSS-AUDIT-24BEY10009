I believe that software should serve people, not corporations.

Every day, I rely on linux — a tool built not by a single
company seeking profit, but by developers across the world who
believed that the knowledge they had should be shared freely.
That choice changed computing. It changed my world.

To me, freedom in software means free coding. Not just the freedom
to use a program, but the freedom to understand it, question it,
fix it, and pass it on better than you found it. This is what
the GPL v2 — the license that governs the Linux kernel itself —
was designed to protect. It is not merely a legal document; it
is a statement that human knowledge belongs to humanity.

If I could build anything and release it to the world, I would
build my ideas — and I would release it under an open license.
Because the greatest software in history was not made to be
locked away. The Linux kernel, Python, Git, Firefox — all of
them grew because someone chose openness over ownership.

I stand on the shoulders of giants. I intend to add to that
height, not wall it off.
